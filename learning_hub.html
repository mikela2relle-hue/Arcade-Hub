‚úÖ Pro Tip: Expanded Form

We can decompose (break apart) a number to see its value:

$873 = 800 + 70 + 3$

To compose (put together) the number, you just add the pieces up!

üìè Comparing Numbers (B1.2 & B1.3)

We use special signs to see which number is bigger, smaller, or the same:

  • > (Greater Than) → The big mouth eats the bigger number!
  • < (Less Than) → The small point points to the smaller number.
  • = (Equal To) → They are exactly the same size.

How to Compare: Always start with the hundreds place first!

‚úÖ Mission: Rounding

Rounding means finding the closest 10 or 100 to make the number easier to work with.

  • If the digit next door is 5 or more (5, 6, 7, 8, 9), you round up! (e.g., 57 → 60)
  • If the digit next door is 4 or less (4, 3, 2, 1, 0), you round down! (e.g., 53 → 50)

üèÉ Skip Counting (B1.4)

‚ûï B2: The Power of Operations

These are your tools for solving problems: adding, taking away, multiplying, and sharing!

üß† Mental Math Secrets (B2.3)

Before you use a pencil, try to estimate!

  • Estimation: Round the big numbers first, then solve. This helps you check if your final answer is reasonable.
  • Example: To solve $512 + 389$, round it to $500 + 400 = 900$. Your real answer should be close to 900!

‚úñÔ∏è Multiplication & Division (B2.2 & B2.6)

You need to know your Math Facts for the numbers 2, 5, and 10!

  • Multiplication ($\times$) is fast adding. ($5 \times 4 = 5 + 5 + 5 + 5$)
  • Division ($\div$) is fair sharing.
    • Example: $30 \div 5 = 6$ (30 cookies shared among 5 friends means 6 cookies each).

‚úÖ Inverse Secret (B2.1): Multiplication and division are opposites!

If $5 \times 6 = 30$, then $30 \div 5 = 6$.

üç∞ Fractions (B1.6 & B1.7)

Fractions are about fair shares!

  • Unit Fractions: Fractions where the top number (numerator) is 1. We use these to talk about equal pieces.
    • Example: $\frac{1}{4}$ means 1 piece out of 4 total pieces.
  • Equivalent Fractions: These are fractions that look different but have the same size!
    • Example: $\frac{1}{2}$ is the same as $\frac{2}{4}$ (they cover the same amount of pizza!).

üé® C1: Pattern Detectives

Everything in math has a pattern! Your job is to spot the rule and keep the pattern going.

‚û°Ô∏è Types of Patterns

  1. Repeating: The same rule happens over and over.
    • Example: $2, 5, 2, 5, 2, \ldots$ (Rule: $+3, -3, +3, -3$)
  2. Growing: The numbers get bigger by adding or multiplying the same amount each time.
    • Example: $10, 20, 30, 40, \ldots$ (Rule: Add 10 each time)
  3. Shrinking: The numbers get smaller by subtracting the same amount each time.
    • Example: $500, 400, 300, \ldots$ (Rule: Subtract 100 each time)
